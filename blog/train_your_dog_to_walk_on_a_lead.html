Train Your Dog to Walk on a Lead

It is hugely important that you train your puppy or dog to walk on a lead with no pulling. A dog that pulls on lead is a danger to itself and to its owner. Training your dog to walk on lead can be a daunting task but if you follow our guide below, it will make the task easier and ensure successful training.

When should I start to train my dog to walk on a lead?

You can start to train a puppy to walk on lead from the age of 8 weeks and upwards. This is an ideal time to train your puppy as it is still in its learning phase and is receptive. It also hasn’t the strength of an adult dog so correcting is easier.

Don’t worry if your dog is older - you can still teach them how to walk on lead effectively. It may take more time and strength but it is achievable. Dogs love to please their owners so training them is easy.

How to reward good behaviour when training?

When training your dog or puppy you have three options for rewarding them:

  • Praise: some dogs respond best to praise and a pat on the head. A simple yet effective form of reward. This is usually the best method when training a puppy.
  • Food: some dogs respond best to food as a reward. Have a packet of treats at hand to reward good behaviour.
  • Toys: some dogs respond well to toys and play as a reward. Have their favourite toy to hand and reward them with this for good behaviour.

What kind of lead do I need?

It is best to use a standard collar (around the neck) and a standard lead. You don’t need any special equipment. Some people think a harness is best but this has been proven wrong. A harness gives the dog more strength to pull from as it can use its whole body as opposed to its neck. The best collar and lead combo is the standard kind.

How to: Lead Training

  • Firstly you must choose the perfect spot to begin your training. It is best to choose an area that is quiet with no distractions. Make sure the area you choose has plenty of space for you to walk in a straight line with your dog or puppy.
  • Put your collar and lead on your dog. Make sure two fingers can fit underneath the collar - this ensures it is neither too tight nor too loose.
  • Choose which side you would like your dog to walk on - your left side or right side. Get your dog in this position.
  • Make sure there is slack in the lead and that it creates a J shape at your side.
  • When you are ready to walk on, give your dog a command - ‘come’, ‘walk’, ‘go’ etc.
  • Take two to three steps forward and stop.
  • If your dog pulls ahead then reverse two to three steps backwards.
  • Dogs want to continue moving forward so reversing teaches them that what they did was wrong.
  • Continue doing this until your dog stops when you stop and doesn’t pull. Make sure to reward this good behaviour with vocal praise first and a treat/toy if necessary.
  • Congratulations, you dog/puppy is now listening to you and paying attention.
  • Continue this exercise each day until you are confident that your dog/puppy has got the hang of it.
  • You can also teach your dog the command of Stop once he/she has got the hang of walking on lead. As you are walking, come to a halt and say ‘Stop’. Your dog will associate this word with coming to a halt - this is an excellent command for dogs that are brought for walks in urban and rural locations. It gives you extra control and peace of mind.
